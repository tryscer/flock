**ai**: In American English, the standard rules for title capitalization (often called "Title Case") generally follow these guidelines:

### The Core Rule
Capitalize the first and last word of the title, regardless of their part of speech. Capitalize all other words **except** for the following categories, unless they are the first or last word:

### Words to Keep Lowercase
- **Articles:** a, an, the.
- **Coordinating Conjunctions:** and, but, for, or, nor, so, yet.
- **Prepositions:** Short prepositions (usually four letters or fewer) such as at, by, for, in, of, on, to, up, with. 
    - *Note:* Some style guides (like APA or Chicago) suggest capitalizing prepositions of five letters or more (e.g., "Between," "Through," "Against").

### Exceptions
- **Always capitalize:** Nouns, pronouns, verbs, adjectives, and adverbs.
- **Always capitalize:** Any word that follows a colon or a dash within the title (as it is considered the start of a subtitle).

### Examples
- *The Catcher in the Rye* ("in" and "the" are lowercase)
- *The Old Man and the Sea* ("and" and "the" are lowercase)
- *Waiting for Godot* ("for" is lowercase)

## Sources
- *The Chicago Manual of Style* (17th ed.)
- *APA Style Guide* (7th ed.)

**ai**: "Is" should **always be capitalized** in titles.

It is a form of the verb "to be." According to standard American English style guides (such as Chicago, APA, and MLA), all verbs—including linking verbs, auxiliary verbs, and forms of "to be" (is, am, are, was, were, be, being, been)—must be capitalized in titles, regardless of their length.
