Mama is Mama Bettye Davis, the UR’s representative, answering to Dr. Player. The UR is not just a vague youth network or caravan-adjacent underground; canon defines it as a clandestine civil-rights movement inspired by the Underground Railroad and by Dr. King’s distinction between desegregation and substantive integration. Mama intentionally formed the Steam Pillars as an instrument of that strategy: not merely to perform, but to create situations in which Black and white youths can actually share joy, space, and experience together. That matters because it makes her not band staff, not just organizer, but architect.[[project-facts]]

What Alice and Tim plausibly perceive, though, is much narrower. From their limited access, Mama is the visible operator attached to the band who seems to have plans bigger than the show. In [[Track 9 - Market Street Stroll]], she is already the one channeling collective energy into action, briefing the kids on how to “lure people into the Love.” Earlier evidence from [[Track 6 - Birds of a Feather]] suggests the UR has distributed personnel and covert reach. Later material indicates it is large, secretive, and resourced. But canon now gives the deeper frame: Mama is trying to use music as a practical integration machine, and the tragedy of her arc is that the machine works too well and then escapes the organization that built it.[[Track 6 - Birds of a Feather]] [[Track 9 - Market Street Stroll]] [[Track 15 - Are You Ready, Atlanta?]] [[project-facts]]

That last part is the real structural insight for your beat map: if “UR” becomes “Mama,” her arc is not simply “civil-rights organizer helps kids.” It is:
- she builds the Pillars as a controlled intervention,
- the intervention succeeds,
- success generates money, attention, and mass flocking,
- that growth weakens UR control,
- Mama is forced from designer of events into manager of consequences.

Seen through Alice and Tim, this should probably arrive as a progression of impressions:
- Mama seems competent.
- Then weirdly connected.
- Then politically purposeful.
- Then more constrained and frustrated than the kids realized.
- Then partially displaced by the very movement she midwifed.

Bluntly: the useful dramatic shape is not “Mama reveals the UR.” It’s “Alice and Tim slowly realize Mama is losing custody of her own miracle.”
