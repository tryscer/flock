# **Rock ’n’ Roll Road Fans in the 1950s–60s: How They Survived on Tour**

## **Transportation on the Road**

Early rock fans often became nomads, chasing concerts by any means available. Hitchhiking emerged as a common mode by the mid-to-late 1960s, when thumbing rides was seen as a “hip” way for restless youth to travel  
[history.com](https://www.history.com/articles/hitchhiking-rise-fall-united-states#:~:text=Once%20the%20war%20ended%20and,to%20get%20to%20antiwar%20protests)  
. A generation of young adults took to the highways with their thumbs out, finding it an adventurous (if unpredictable) way to get from show to show. For every thrilling free ride, however, there were long stretches of walking and waiting – hitchhikers recall plenty of nights sleeping in roadside bushes when no one would stop  
[sandiegoreader.com](https://www.sandiegoreader.com/news/1989/may/11/cover-theres-only-one-hitch/#:~:text=Every%20hitchhiker%20has%20a%20story,great%20hitchhiking%20adventure%20lives%20on)  
. Traveling in pairs or groups was especially common for young women, both for safety and company. One trio of 1960s “hippie” girls, for example, hitched from Vancouver to Mexico and back, camping by the road at night and sticking together to ward off dangerous rides  
[sandiegoreader.com](https://www.sandiegoreader.com/news/1989/may/11/cover-theres-only-one-hitch/#:~:text=Standing%20on%20the%20La%20Costa,seen%20anything%20like%20them%20before)  
[sandiegoreader.com](https://www.sandiegoreader.com/news/1989/may/11/cover-theres-only-one-hitch/#:~:text=%E2%80%9CDon%E2%80%99t%20see%20many%20women%20hitchhiking,anymore%2C%E2%80%9D%20I%20said)  
.Besides hitchhiking, fans utilized buses and shared cars extensively. In the 1950s, dedicated teenagers might save up for a Greyhound ticket to follow their idol’s tour. Kentucky teenager Bobbie Ann Mason recalls her first 16-hour overnight bus ride – chaperoned by her mother – to see a favorite vocal group perform in Cincinnati  
[newyorker.com](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/1986/05/26/reaching-the-stars-my-life-as-a-fifties-groupie#:~:text=IN%20time%2C%20my%20mother%20and,suburban%20dance%20hall%20that%20was)  
. The pair later drove their family’s Nash Rambler across multiple states (Illinois, Arkansas, Missouri and more) to catch shows, essentially doing regional “road trips” whenever the band toured nearby  
[newyorker.com](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/1986/05/26/reaching-the-stars-my-life-as-a-fifties-groupie#:~:text=AFTER%20that%2C%20Mama%20and%20I,They%20took%20a)  
. Not every fan had supportive parents with a car, of course. Many piled into beat-up cars or vans with friends, splitting gas money to caravan from gig to gig. By the late ’60s, crisscrossing the country in a VW microbus full of like-minded music fans was almost a rite of passage for the rock counterculture. Train-hopping was less common (riding the rails was an older hobo tradition), but a few resourceful fans might take cheap local trains if available. Air travel was largely out of reach – few teens could afford plane tickets, so following a band meant hitting the road.

## **Sleeping Arrangements**

Stretching a dollar on the road meant getting creative with lodging. Fans with money (or indulgent parents) could grab a cheap motel room near the concert venue – the burgeoning postwar motel industry made roadside rooms relatively affordable. More often, young travelers slept wherever they could. It was not unusual to spend a night in one’s car at a rest stop or parking lot. Others unrolled sleeping bags in fields, parks, or behind gas stations – any spot that felt semi-safe for a few hours of shuteye. “For every lucky ride,” one hitchhiker noted, there might be “half a dozen nights spent sleeping in the bushes” when you’re stranded between towns  
[sandiegoreader.com](https://www.sandiegoreader.com/news/1989/may/11/cover-theres-only-one-hitch/#:~:text=Every%20hitchhiker%20has%20a%20story,great%20hitchhiking%20adventure%20lives%20on)  
.By the late 1960s, urban music hubs developed informal “crash pads” for transient youth. In San Francisco’s Haight-Ashbury (epicenter of the 1967 Summer of Love), community activists called the Diggers actually organized free communal shelters. They “set up crash pads to offer free shelter” to the influx of young music fans arriving with “no practical sense of how to survive”  
[sfchronicle.com](https://www.sfchronicle.com/entertainment/article/Diggers-fed-the-masses-with-free-as-their-10987583.php#:~:text=The%20organizers%20of%20the%20feast,how%20to%20survive%20the%20experience)  
[sfchronicle.com](https://www.sfchronicle.com/entertainment/article/Diggers-fed-the-masses-with-free-as-their-10987583.php#:~:text=%E2%80%9CFree%E2%80%9D%20was%20the%20Diggers%E2%80%99%20mantra,a%20hotel%20into%20free%20housing)  
. In these crash houses, dozens of wandering kids might share floors and couches, grateful for a roof. Elsewhere, fans relied on hospitality networks: often you could couch-surf with fellow fans or kind strangers. Fan club connections helped – 1950s fan-magazine presidents sometimes hosted out-of-town devotees. Mason, for instance, visited fellow fan club leaders in other cities during her travels (staying with them when possible)  
[newyorker.com](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/1986/05/26/reaching-the-stars-my-life-as-a-fifties-groupie#:~:text=During%20my%20years%20with%20the,on%20Soupy%20Sales%E2%80%99%20original%20TV)  
.Some die-hards finagled their way into the same hotels as the band. The most devoted (often young women known as groupies) became masters at sneaking in. They would scour newspapers for a band’s hotel, then loiter in lobbies and even “pad around the hotel corridors” hoping to be invited to a room  
[time.com](https://time.com/archive/6636777/manners-and-morals-the-groupies/#:~:text=Their%20appeal%20is%20obvious,%E2%80%9D%20Josephine%20Mori%2C%20public)  
. If successful, a groupie might crash in a star’s suite (or a roadie’s room) for the night – a free bed as well as bragging rights. Those less lucky might bed down in a friendly fan’s dorm room or even camp out near the concert hall. Campgrounds were an option in warm weather; some fans carried tents or slept under the stars at campsites between shows. The accommodations were rarely glamorous – one British Invasion-era fan quipped that following a tour meant learning to “sleep sitting up” in cars, buses, or whatever shelter one could find  
[sandiegoreader.com](https://www.sandiegoreader.com/news/1989/may/11/cover-theres-only-one-hitch/#:~:text=Her%20friends%20in%20back%20had,and%20hadn%E2%80%99t%20gotten%20much%20sleep)  
[sandiegoreader.com](https://www.sandiegoreader.com/news/1989/may/11/cover-theres-only-one-hitch/#:~:text=When%20I%20pulled%20off%20on,heading%20into%20a%20Burger%20King)  
. In short, rock devotees made do with motels when they could, and cars, couches or grass when they couldn’t.

## **Eating on the Go**

Road food for rock fans was a catch-as-catch-can affair. Diners and truck stops dotted every highway and became default pit stops. A classic 24-hour diner could provide a hot meal and a bottomless cup of coffee for cheap – heaven after a long drive or an overnight hitchhiking wait in the rain. Many lived on fast food; as the 1960s progressed, national chains like McDonald’s and Burger King spread, offering quick calories to go. One anecdote from 1960s California describes three weary girl hitchhikers, after a night on the roadside, happily heading into a Burger King for breakfast once they reached the next town  
[sandiegoreader.com](https://www.sandiegoreader.com/news/1989/may/11/cover-theres-only-one-hitch/#:~:text=When%20I%20pulled%20off%20on,heading%20into%20a%20Burger%20King)  
. Concert days often meant splurging on junk food at whatever drive-in was near the venue, or pooling money for pizzas and hamburgers.Skipping meals was not uncommon either – excitement (or lack of cash) could suppress appetite. Bobbie Ann Mason recalled being so excited on her first trip that she “couldn’t eat” at all before the show  
[newyorker.com](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/1986/05/26/reaching-the-stars-my-life-as-a-fifties-groupie#:~:text=IN%20time%2C%20my%20mother%20and,suburban%20dance%20hall%20that%20was)  
. More budget-conscious travelers packed sustenance from home: loaves of bread, peanut butter, canned beans, and spam were staples that didn’t spoil easily. Some carried coolers to picnic on the cheap. In communal crash pads or group houses, fans often cooked together. A big pot of stew or spaghetti could feed a dozen hungry drifters for just a few dollars of ingredients. By late 1966–67, San Francisco’s Diggers took this to another level by organizing free daily meals. They handed out flyers announcing “Free food… Bring a bowl and spoon” every afternoon in the Panhandle park  
[sfchronicle.com](https://www.sfchronicle.com/entertainment/article/Diggers-fed-the-masses-with-free-as-their-10987583.php#:~:text=One%20day%20in%20October%201966%2C,%E2%80%9D)  
. Huge vats of donated stew and “Digger bread” were served to hundreds of young music lovers, no payment needed  
[sfchronicle.com](https://www.sfchronicle.com/entertainment/article/Diggers-fed-the-masses-with-free-as-their-10987583.php#:~:text=The%20organizers%20of%20the%20feast,how%20to%20survive%20the%20experience)  
[sfchronicle.com](https://www.sfchronicle.com/entertainment/article/Diggers-fed-the-masses-with-free-as-their-10987583.php#:~:text=Then%20the%20Diggers%20would%20set,cans%2C%20to%20distribute%20as%20well)  
. This ethos of communal kitchens spread – improvised “free food” tables popped up at music festivals and in hippie neighborhoods, ensuring penniless fans didn’t starve.On the flip side, some better-heeled fans dined well. A lucky few got taken out by band members after shows – Mason and her mom, for example, were treated to strawberry sundaes and cafe hamburgers by the grateful singers they befriended  
[newyorker.com](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/1986/05/26/reaching-the-stars-my-life-as-a-fifties-groupie#:~:text=%E2%80%9CWell%2C%20gee%2C%20Mrs,%E2%80%9D)  
[newyorker.com](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/1986/05/26/reaching-the-stars-my-life-as-a-fifties-groupie#:~:text=%E2%80%9CWhat%20do%20you%20think%20of,to%20a%20caf%C3%A9%20for%20hamburgers)  
. But those cases were exceptional. Typically, a touring fan’s diet was heavy on greasy spoon fare and light on nutrition. Hunger was an ever-present companion if money ran low. Notably, black fans on the road faced additional challenges in the 1950s–early ’60s: segregation meant they couldn’t even patronize many restaurants. African American travelers often had to “feed themselves all the way”, bringing homemade food in the car rather than risk stopping at whites-only diners  
[traveler.marriott.com](https://traveler.marriott.com/black-travel/lawrence-ross-modern-day-travels-with-the-green-book/#:~:text=Ross%20says%20that%20his%20mother,that%20lined%20the%20small%20roadways)  
. Guides like the *Negro Motorist Green Book* listed the rare black-friendly cafés and lunch counters  
[traveler.marriott.com](https://traveler.marriott.com/black-travel/lawrence-ross-modern-day-travels-with-the-green-book/#:~:text=Imagine%20you%20were%20traveling%20across,Americans)  
 – otherwise, a black fan driving across the South to see, say, Little Richard might pack a shoebox of fried chicken and biscuits from home. For white fans at least, highway food was readily accessible; for fans of color, simply finding a meal without humiliation required extra planning.

## **Bathing and Laundry**

Keeping clean on a nonstop concert trek was tricky but not impossible. Resourceful fans found ways to wash up in transit. Gas station restrooms doubled as makeshift washrooms – a fan could slip into the ladies’ or men’s room with a washcloth, soap, and maybe dry shampoo to freshen up. (Many learned the art of a “sink shower,” using the faucet and paper towels to clean the essentials.) Larger truck stops by the late ’60s offered coin-operated public showers for long-haul drivers – these became a godsend for grubby hitchhikers as well. With a few quarters, one could get a hot shower and towel at an interstate truck plaza. Fans also availed themselves of YMCA/YWCA facilities in cities; for a modest fee, YMCAs provided access to showers and sometimes even a cheap bed for the night, a refuge used by many young travelers of the era.If a fan scored a night in a motel, they certainly took full advantage of the private bath. One 30-something drifter described arriving after days on the road still “neatly groomed” and “smelling of soap,” a hint that he had managed to scrub up somewhere before catching his next ride  
[sandiegoreader.com](https://www.sandiegoreader.com/news/1989/may/11/cover-theres-only-one-hitch/#:~:text=%E2%80%9CWell%2C%20climb%20in%2C%E2%80%9D%20I%20said)  
. Rivers, lakes, and beach fronts also became ad hoc bathtubs – a quick dip could substitute for a shower in a pinch (a common trick for festival-goers camping out). Laundry was another challenge: wearing clothes multiple days was standard, but eventually one had to wash them. Coin laundromats were booming in mid-century; traveling fans would pop into a laundromat in whatever town they passed, washing clothes while grabbing a bite at the diner next door. In a communal crash pad setting, clothes might be washed in a shared load or simply hand-scrubbed in a bathtub. Some enterprising young women even traded domestic skills for hospitality – e.g. doing a load of laundry for a local host in exchange for use of their shower.For groupies traveling with bands, hygiene could be both easier and harder. Easier because they had access to hotels (and hot showers) when tagging along with the crew, harder because life on a tour bus or crowded motel meant little privacy and irregular hours. Bands famously didn’t bathe often on tour, and their devoted followers sometimes adopted the same gritty look. Still, by necessity, fans found ways to stay semi-clean – even if that meant a sponge bath in a gas station or using dry shampoo and perfume to mask the road dirt. As one memoirist put it, “roughing it” was part of the adventure; a bit of grime was a small price to pay for rock & roll glory.

## **Making Ends Meet: Money and Hustles**

Funding a fan’s wandering lifestyle required hustle, creativity, or sheer thrift. Some young fans saved up beforehand – working summer jobs, stashing babysitting money, or skimping on school lunches to build a concert fund. A frugal teen in 1963 could finance a few bus tickets and cheap show tickets with enough scrimping. Once on the road, however, money often ran short, and that’s where ingenuity and grit kicked in. Many picked up odd jobs along the way. It wasn’t unheard of for a fan to wash dishes at a diner for a night, do farm work for a day, or busk with a guitar on a street corner to earn gas money. Handy travelers offered to pump gas or clean windshields for tips at service stations. In music towns, some hung around venues and got hired for quick gigs like stacking chairs or helping roadies load equipment (earning a few dollars or at least free entry to the show).Others resorted to panhandling – essentially relying on the kindness of strangers. Especially in the late ’60s hippie era, young people with “Please help, stranded traveler” signs were a common sight. Many music fans did not shy from asking for spare change to “get to the next show,” and often fellow fans or kindly strangers would oblige with small donations. This blurs into scams at times. One famous ploy involved carrying an empty gas can: as one hitchhiker noted, motorists who would never pick up a hitchhiker *would* stop for someone apparently out of gas – and he used this trick to snag rides across the country  
[sandiegoreader.com](https://www.sandiegoreader.com/news/1989/may/11/cover-theres-only-one-hitch/#:~:text=The%20smartest%20hitchhiker%20I%20ever,because%20they%20admired%20his%20ingenuity)  
. Fans short on cash sometimes fibbed or schemed similarly, perhaps claiming a fictitious emergency to solicit a few bucks. Some became adept at sneaking into concerts to save money, forging tickets or sweet-talking their way past doormen. A few bold souls even bribed their way into better situations; during the groupie heyday, girls would bribe hotel security guards to let them into band suites  
[time.com](https://time.com/archive/6636777/manners-and-morals-the-groupies/#:~:text=Crash%20Course,them%20into%20stars%E2%80%99%20hotel%20rooms)  
 (not with much money – usually with flirtation or perhaps a shared joint).Indeed, drugs and sex became currencies of survival on the road. A number of enterprising fans financed their travels by dealing a little marijuana or LSD at concerts – rock tours and festivals were fertile ground for amateur drug sales. Young women in the groupie scene, especially, leveraged sexual relationships for sustenance. Many groupies vehemently insisted “we were not prostitutes”  
[theguardian.com](https://www.theguardian.com/music/2015/nov/15/groupies-revisited-baron-wolman-rolling-stone-pamela-des-barres#:~:text=It%E2%80%99s%20a%20word%20infused%20with,uncomfortable%20with%20anything%20and%20was)  
, because their intimacy with musicians was (in their view) about love of music and adventure, not cash. Still, the line could blur. A devoted groupie might not ask for money, but she often received gifts, meals, even housing from the star or crew she “mused” for. Less romantically, some drifting young women did engage in occasional sex work – if you were broke in a strange city, exchanging favors for a place to crash or for a few dollars could be a last resort. This was by no means every fan’s story, but it lurked as a harsh reality for some. One infamous groupie, Cherry Vanilla, later quipped about “heroically” performing oral sex around the music industry to help her favorite artist’s career  
[blowoutmagazine.com](https://www.blowoutmagazine.com/other-stuff/2018/10/23/road-wives-the-evolution-of-the-groupie#:~:text=From%20gorgeous%20and%20celebrated%20groupie,Even%20famous%20feminist%20Germaine%20Greer)  
 – a shockingly candid example of how sex was sometimes treated as a tool of the trade. In short, survival demanded hustle. As one 33-year-old former super-groupie bitterly tallied, after years on the road she had “three kids from three different guys… been busted twice” and little to show for it  
[time.com](https://time.com/archive/6636777/manners-and-morals-the-groupies/#:~:text=For%20every%20such%20success%20story%2C,%E2%80%9D)  
. The lucky or savvy ones parlayed their adventures into marriages, memoirs, or music business jobs; others ended up nearly destitute.

## **Who Were These Traveling Fans? (Demographics and Social Gaps)**

In the 1950s and early ’60s, the prototypical roaming rock fan was young – often very young – and usually female. The mass hysterias of that era (from Sinatra’s bobby-soxers in the ’40s to Elvis Presley’s screaming teenyboppers in the ’50s) were overwhelmingly driven by teenage girls  
[time.com](https://time.com/archive/6636777/manners-and-morals-the-groupies/#:~:text=S%20tage,their%20worship%20knows%20no%20bounds)  
. Many were middle-class high schoolers intoxicated by the new rock ’n’ roll freedom. Some were so determined that they ran away from home or convinced their parents to chaperone them to distant shows (as Bobbie Mason did). By the mid-1960s, with the rise of the term “groupie,” the age range widened, including college-age women and a smattering of older “scene chasers.” Frank Zappa observed in 1969 that rock groupies ranged “all the way from 50…down to ten” years old (the extremes being rare) and came from essentially any background exposed to rock music  
[time.com](https://time.com/archive/6636777/manners-and-morals-the-groupies/#:~:text=Class%20Strata,down%20to%20ten)  
[time.com](https://time.com/archive/6636777/manners-and-morals-the-groupies/#:~:text=rock%20stars%2C%20like%20most%20young,down%20to%20ten)  
. In practice, most were in their late teens or early twenties, with a few precocious younger teens and a few hangers-on in their thirties. Notably, gender roles were at play: young women could more easily integrate themselves into a touring scene (bands welcomed pretty girls), whereas young men who followed bands tended to do so in support roles (road crew, aspiring musicians) rather than as “fans” per se. Societal norms made it tougher for a teenage girl to hitchhike solo or crash in a strange city – those who did so were often labeled “wild” or delinquent – yet many did it anyway, forming girl groups for safety. The trio of hitchhiking girls in 1969 California, for example, explicitly said they felt safe as three and still “turned down a lot of rides” that felt sketchy  
[sandiegoreader.com](https://www.sandiegoreader.com/news/1989/may/11/cover-theres-only-one-hitch/#:~:text=%E2%80%9CDon%E2%80%99t%20see%20many%20women%20hitchhiking,anymore%2C%E2%80%9D%20I%20said)  
. Female fans had to be street-smart and careful, balancing their hunger for adventure with the ever-present risk of harassment or worse. Male fans, while less discussed, certainly existed – think of the early rock “greasers” or rockabilly enthusiasts who might road-trip to see a hot new guitar picker. But a carload of guys chasing a band drew less notice (and perhaps less scorn) than the packs of hitchhiking girls who became emblematic of the era.Race and class hugely affected who could live this vagabond fan lifestyle. In the 1950s, African American youth loved rock ’n’ roll just as much (the music itself sprang from Black R\&B), but segregation imposed harsh limits on their mobility. A Black fan driving across Jim Crow America to follow a tour faced hotels and restaurants that would turn them away due to skin color  
[traveler.marriott.com](https://traveler.marriott.com/black-travel/lawrence-ross-modern-day-travels-with-the-green-book/#:~:text=Imagine%20you%20were%20traveling%20across,Americans)  
[traveler.marriott.com](https://traveler.marriott.com/black-travel/lawrence-ross-modern-day-travels-with-the-green-book/#:~:text=Ross%20says%20that%20his%20mother,that%20lined%20the%20small%20roadways)  
. Unless they stuck to the “Chitlin’ Circuit” venues and made use of the Green Book guide (which listed Black-friendly lodgings), Black rock fans in the South had to travel in groups or with church-sponsored outings, or not at all. The risk of racist violence in unfamiliar towns was a powerful deterrent. Meanwhile, white fans – especially those from working or middle-class families – had far more leeway to roam. Class determined how comfortably one roamed: kids from solid middle-class homes might have a car, allowance, or safety net to tap. They could afford bus tickets, motel rooms, and the occasional restaurant meal. Many early fan club leaders, like Mason, were small-town or middle-class girls who could at least manage periodic trips with parental help  
[newyorker.com](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/1986/05/26/reaching-the-stars-my-life-as-a-fifties-groupie#:~:text=AFTER%20that%2C%20Mama%20and%20I,They%20took%20a)  
. In contrast, poorer fans or runaway kids traveled on a shoestring: hitchhiking out of necessity, sleeping rough, and scraping by day to day. Ironically, a subset of groupies in the late ’60s were actually quite affluent – the “Super Groupies” were “often well-heeled” young women from comfortable backgrounds  
[time.com](https://time.com/archive/6636777/manners-and-morals-the-groupies/#:~:text=Super%20Status,after%E2%80%94company)  
. These few could fly to London or L.A. and stay in nice hotels, essentially glamorous tourists in the rock scene. But they were the exception. The typical band-following fan had modest means and accepted discomfort as the price of admission.Gender expectations also meant that the freedom to drift was unequally distributed. A young man could thumb cross-country and society might call him a beatnik or adventurer; a young woman doing the same was more likely to be labeled irresponsible or “fast.” Still, trailblazing girls defied the norms. Many recall that in the freewheeling ’60s, being a rock fan on the road was a great equalizer – sleeping in the same campgrounds, eating the same cheap food, becoming like a little tribe on the concert trail regardless of where you came from. That said, by 1970 there was a backlash: even within the fan scene, a division arose between the so-called “Super Fans” (who prided themselves on protecting their idols from sexually aggressive groupies) and the groupies themselves  
[time.com](https://time.com/archive/6636777/manners-and-morals-the-groupies/#:~:text=Most%20groupies%20may%20be%20luckier,often%20seem%20to%20be%20appreciated)  
. This hinted at a growing stigma around the young women who had been at the center of the traveling circus.In summary, the fans who followed rock bands across regions in the ’50s and ’60s were primarily young, adventurous, and resourceful – often teenage girls or college-aged dropouts chasing a musical high. They learned to survive on little money and lots of moxie, hitching rides, crashing wherever, subsisting on hamburgers and generosity. Those from privileged backgrounds had an easier ride (and sometimes even attained a measure of insider status), while those without means lived truly hand-to-mouth. And whereas white youth could thumb around with relative impunity (aside from the usual perils of the road), Black youth had to navigate a minefield of segregation that made such carefree movement perilous  
[traveler.marriott.com](https://traveler.marriott.com/black-travel/lawrence-ross-modern-day-travels-with-the-green-book/#:~:text=Imagine%20you%20were%20traveling%20across,Americans)  
[traveler.marriott.com](https://traveler.marriott.com/black-travel/lawrence-ross-modern-day-travels-with-the-green-book/#:~:text=Ross%20says%20that%20his%20mother,that%20lined%20the%20small%20roadways)  
. Gender cut both ways: young women were the most visible traveling fans – the iconic screaming girls and peace-and-love hippie chicks – and they leveraged their femininity to get closer to the music, yet they also faced unique dangers on the road that their male counterparts did not. Despite all these differences, what united this ragtag cohort was an almost naive passion. Whether it was 15-year-old girls skipping school to tail Elvis’s tour bus, or a van full of hippies trekking Route 66 to follow the Rolling Stones, these fans shared a fervor for rock ’n’ roll that drove them to extraordinary lengths. ), contemporary journalism like *Time* and *Rolling Stone* on the 1960s groupie phenomenon
