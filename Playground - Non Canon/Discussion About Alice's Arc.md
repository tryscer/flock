Why it works: Side A ends by teaching her that at a Pillars show “you’re not a spectator.”[^6] So her active want can become: join in, contribute, do the thing rather than merely admire it. That bridges neatly from fandom culture in B to revolutionary crowd behavior in C-D.

Why it works: this is a concrete social version of the above. Alice already responds strongly to integrated dancing and public musical contagion.[^7] If her motivation becomes “get people into the dance,” then in B-C she embodies rock’n’roll as social dissolution; in C-D that same instinct becomes quasi-insurrectionary mass action.

- **Earn usefulness inside the collective**
Why it works: this solves your agency problem. Alice can want not just to belong, but to be useful: carry messages, recruit, soothe, teach, help stage actions, later learn drums. Canon already points toward her musical tutelage and later practical role.[^8] It keeps her from being only the receiver of revelation.

- **Follow the band so completely that following becomes living**
Why it works: this fits your point that she should not be “different” from the others but the ideal type of them. Early on, she wants another show; by Side B that can mature into wanting the caravan life itself, as the normal form of life.[^9] That gives B-C the culture of fandom-on-the-road, and by D it can evolve into loyalty to the flock beyond the band.

My blunt recommendation: make her main B-side motivation a fusion of **missionary + participant**:

That is better than “join the movement,” because she’s already in. It is also better than “follow Tim,” because it keeps her arc centered on the collective current that later outgrows romance.
