## Research around the research station
Columbia is the state capital and sits at the center of some of the richest Black-institutional and Reconstruction history in the South. 
## 1. The local anchor (Columbia proper)

**The Waverly neighborhood** — this is your emotional foundation. Waverly was one of the first planned Black suburbs in South Carolina, home to Black doctors, lawyers, teachers, and business owners, anchored by two HBCUs sitting side by side: **Allen University** (AME) and **Benedict College** (Baptist). By 1958 this is a real, thriving, self-sufficient Black professional world — exactly the "come here to breathe" texture you want for a haven town after Faith. Your characters wouldn't just hide in Columbia; they'd find a *class* of educated, landed, unbowed Black people running their own institutions.

**Bonus flavor — the Big Apple:** the "Big Apple" dance was born at a Black nightclub in Columbia (a converted synagogue on Park Street) in the mid-1930s, then swept the nation. For a novel called *Flock Music*, a haven town that also happens to be where a dance craze *escaped Black culture into the mainstream* is thematically gold — the same "Black origin, national theft" rhyme rock'n'roll runs on.

## 2. The commune / research-station model (your best SC find)

**"Promised Land," SC (Greenwood County)** — this is almost too perfect. During Reconstruction, the **South Carolina Land Commission (1869)** bought up land and resold it in small plots to freedpeople. One settlement, in Greenwood County ~60 miles from Columbia, is *literally named* Promised Land — a community of Black landowners who held their acreage across generations despite intense white hostility. It's the closest real SC structural match to a self-governing, land-owning Black collective. Steal the origin logic wholesale: *the state itself once handed these families land, and they never let go of it.*

Layer that with:
- **South Carolina State (Orangeburg)** — SC's Black land-grant "1890" college, ~40 miles from Columbia, running a segregated Black agricultural extension service. Your research-station founder is credibly an SC State man (or Tuskegee-trained) who came home to build something.
- **Penn Center, St. Helena Island** — Gullah cooperative land-holding and training/retreat hub (later a civil-rights nerve center). Good for the "haven that's also a meeting-place" function.
## 3. Why flowers — and why it works in the SC Midlands

The Midlands and sandhills soil around Columbia genuinely supports ornamental horticulture, which lets you make flowers *load-bearing* rather than decorative:

- **Camellias** — the Columbia/Aiken/Sumter belt was a serious camellia and nursery region mid-century; camellia societies and named cultivars were a real thing. A Black research station breeding its own camellia and gladiolus varieties is period-plausible *and* a gorgeous metaphor — patient, generational work, cultivating something lasting and their own.
- **Economic cleverness** — cut flowers, gladioli, and bulbs could ship north by rail into markets that didn't run through white-controlled cotton gins and tobacco warehouses. That's the *reason* they chose flowers: independence from the sharecropping/lien system. Beauty as a path out of debt peonage.
- **Beauty as resistance** — a sundown town of violence a few miles off, and a haven that grows *flowers* in the same red clay that grows terror. The contrast writes itself.

(One real-world spiritual cousin worth knowing even though it's later: **Pearl Fryar's topiary garden** in Bishopville, SC — a Black man turning cast-off plants into world-famous art on his own land. Wrong decade to cite, but exactly the *soul* of what you're building.)

## The Big Apple Night Club — Columbia, SC

### The Building

In 1916, the second House of Peace Synagogue was constructed at 1318 Park Street in a downtown Columbia neighborhood that housed a mix of Jewish, Greek, and African-American residents. The synagogue was built to replace an older one that had burned down on the site. It featured the Star of David in stained glass over the door, oak pews, pine floors, and a high balcony to accommodate female worshipers.

The congregation outgrew the building and moved to their third synagogue on 1719 Marion Street in early 1935, after which the building was converted into the Big Apple Club.

### The Founders

The building was sold to H. W. DesPortes, a traveling salesman, and leased and operated as a nightclub by Frank "Fat Sam" Boyd and "Big" Elliott Wright. Boyd, tall, stocky, and only 20, was an avid dancer, and Wright, at 350 pounds and not to be messed with, was a musician, so these interests combined to create a dance-hall feel for their club. A sign featuring a bright red apple was hung above the door, and the Big Apple Night Club was born. Wright and Boyd installed a phonograph upon which Big Band and other jazz tunes could be played for a nickel. Dancers came from the surrounding working-class African-American neighborhoods which filled the blocks below Park Street to the river.

### The Dance

The Big Apple is both a partner dance and a circle dance. One author suggests it originated from the "ring shout," a group dance associated with religious observances founded before 1860 by African Americans on plantations in South Carolina and Georgia — a dance with "counterclockwise circling and high arm gestures" that resembled the Big Apple.

The Big Apple was a combination of the square dance and various jazz routines of the 1920s.

### The Racial Dynamic

In 1936, three white students from the University of South Carolina — Billy Spivey, Donald Davis, and Harold "Goo-Goo" Wiles — heard the music coming from the juke joint as they were driving by. Even though it was very unusual for whites to go into a Black club, the three asked Fat Sam if they could enter. Skip Davis, the son of Donald Davis, said that "Fat Sam made two conditions. They had to pay twenty-five cents each and they had to sit in the balcony."

These white spectators copied the moves and traveled more freely, allowing the dance craze to spread farther and faster.

### Going National

In the summer of 1937, white dancers spread the new dance to the Pavilion in Myrtle Beach. Word got to the manager of the Roxy nightclub in New York City, and scouts came south to recruit white dancers to perform at the Roxy for $50 a day. From there, the dance craze went from coast to coast, inspiring bandleader Tommy Dorsey to write "The Big Apple Swing" and President Roosevelt to host a Big Apple party at the White House. The Big Apple later morphed into the jitterbug, and is considered the foundation of modern rock and roll dancing.

### The Injustice Hidden Inside the Story

Meanwhile, Black dancers in Columbia continued to perform their dance and improvise new moves and sounds, but without the fanfare or paid performances that the white dancers received. The originators of the craze were left behind while white performers profited from it nationally — a pattern repeated countless times in American cultural history with jazz, blues, and rock and roll.

### The Building Today

In the early 1980s, the building was physically moved to its present location at the southeast corner of Hampton and Park Streets. In 1993, it was purchased by the Historic Columbia Foundation and is now called the Big Apple. It is listed on the National Register of Historic Places and is rented out for social events — the pine floors where that history was made still intact.
